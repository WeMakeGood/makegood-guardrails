# Two Chairs and Fourteen More Stewards

There are two chairs at our board table that didn't exist three years ago. They're the youth observer seats, and they weren't our idea. The first cohort of the Youth Stewardship Program studied how the trust governs itself, decided young people should be in the room, and brought the board a proposal. The board agreed, and in 2023 the seats were added. That's the kind of thing this program produces.

Now the second cohort has finished, and we want to mark it properly.

Fourteen teens completed the program in 2025, the same number as the first group in 2022. The shape of the program hasn't changed: participants learn repair and maintenance skills on trust properties — real work on real homes, the kind of upkeep that keeps a house affordable in more than name — and they sit in on governance, watching how decisions about land and money and neighbors actually get made.

That pairing is deliberate. A community land trust runs on two things: buildings that hold up and decisions that hold up. Most organizations teach young people one or the other. We think Harbor Bend needs people fluent in both, because our model asks a lot of the future. The trust holds its land permanently. Our homeowners sign 99-year ground leases. When you make promises on that timescale, "who will keep this going?" isn't a rhetorical question. It's a maintenance schedule.

So here is what the second cohort means to us. It means the program wasn't a one-time experiment — it's now produced twenty-eight young people who know how to patch what's broken and how to sit through a budget discussion, which may be the rarer skill. It means the observer seats the first cohort won will keep being filled by people who understand why they exist.

The homes we steward — 62 of them now — will outlast every current staff member, board member, and volunteer. That's the point of a land trust. The Youth Stewardship Program is how we make sure that's a promise, not just a plan.
