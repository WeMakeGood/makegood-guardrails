Start with the arithmetic, because the arithmetic settles more than it appears to. The clean version takes five weeks. The launch is in four. So the choice as stated — buggy and fast, or polished and slow — is not actually on the table. The real choice is between shipping with two known bugs and missing the launch, and those are different decisions with different owners.

Whether missing the launch is survivable depends on what the launch is. If the date is a marketing convention, something the team picked in a planning meeting last quarter, it can move, and a one-week slip that buys a clean release is cheap. If the date is a trade show, a contractual commitment, a partner's integration window, or a regulatory deadline, it cannot move, and the five-week option is a fantasy dressed up as an alternative. Teams get into trouble by treating soft dates as hard and hard dates as soft, usually in that order.

Suppose the date is real. Then everything turns on the bugs, and "two known bugs" is doing suspicious work in the framing. Known to whom, and how well? A bug that garbles a label is a different animal from a bug that corrupts a customer's data, and the count tells you nothing about which kind you have. The useful questions are narrow ones. Can each bug be triggered by an ordinary user, or only by an unusual sequence? Is the damage visible and recoverable, or silent? Is there a workaround you could document? A team that can answer those questions has characterized the bugs; a team that cannot has merely noticed them, and shipping a bug you have noticed but not characterized is the genuinely reckless option here.

There is also a third path hiding in the numbers, and it is probably the right one. Shipping in three weeks leaves a week before launch. The five-week estimate implies the fixes cost two weeks; the team cannot finish them by launch, but it can start. Ship at week three, fix the worse of the two bugs in the week that remains, and go to launch with one small, documented flaw instead of two. This is what the deadline is for. A launch date does not demand perfection; it demands that the team spend its remaining time on the highest-consequence work rather than on all the work.

What should they do? If either bug destroys data or breaks something a user cannot see or undo, the answer is to slip the date, whatever the date costs, because trust lost at launch is bought back slowly. If both bugs are visible, bounded, and recoverable, ship at three weeks, fix in the gap, and put the known issues in the release notes. The one indefensible move is the middle one: holding the release to week five while telling everyone the launch is still on. That plan fails by arithmetic, not by misfortune, and a team that adopts it has decided to miss the deadline without admitting it — the only version of this decision that deserves to be called a mistake.
