Ask at a dinner party and you will hear two kinds of certainty. The owners will tell you that rent is money thrown away, that every month a renter is paying down someone else's mortgage. The renters will tell you that a house is a leveraged, illiquid, undiversified bet with a roof problem, and that a down payment left in an index fund would have done better. Both sides are answering a different question from the one asked. The question is not whether owning is good in general; it is whether owning is good for a particular person, in a particular place, over a particular stretch of years. Strip away the particulars and what remains is not an answer but a map of where the answer lives.

Start with the arithmetic, because folklore gets it wrong in the owners' favor. Rent is not thrown away. It buys housing, the way grocery money buys food. The owner avoids rent but pays property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and mortgage interest, and none of those build equity either. The honest comparison sets rent against the unrecoverable costs of owning, plus whatever the down payment could have earned invested elsewhere. That comparison turns on one number: the ratio of a home's price to the annual rent it would fetch. Where the ratio is low, around 15 or below, buying tends to win comfortably. Where it climbs past 25, as it has in the most expensive coastal cities, a renter who invests the difference can come out ahead for decades. It is the most useful number in the whole debate, and almost nobody making the largest purchase of their life bothers to compute it.

Then the horizon. Buying and later selling a home consumes something like eight to ten percent of its price in commissions, taxes, and moving costs. Spread over fifteen years, that expense is noise. Spread over three, it can swallow every dollar the house gained and more. This is why the oldest advice in the field, that you should not buy unless you expect to stay at least five years, has survived every boom and bust since it was first given. It also reveals what the question is actually about. "Should I buy?" usually means "Do I know what my life looks like in five years?" A person who cannot answer that is not failing some test of adulthood. They are receiving accurate information about themselves, and acting on it costs far less as a renter than as a seller.

What the spreadsheet holds less well is the case for owning that owners actually feel. A fixed mortgage is rent control you write for yourself: the payment that strains a young couple's budget in year one is, by year fifteen, a bargain no landlord on the street would match. Forced saving is real, too. In principle a renter can invest the difference every month for thirty years; in practice most households will not, and the mortgage collects it anyway. And some goods have no line item at all — the wall you are allowed to knock out, the tree that will be tall when your children are, the certainty that no one can sell the building out from under you. Renting has its own unpriced goods, and they are the same ones inverted. The renter can follow a job across the country in a month, leave a bad landlord or a bad street, and treat a falling market as someone else's problem. A lease is short. That is its cost, and it is also the entire point.

So the answer is conditional, but the conditions are not mysterious. Buy when you can say where you will be in five years, when the local price-to-rent arithmetic is sane, and when the payment leaves room for the rest of a life. Rent when any of those conditions fails, and invest what the mortgage would have taken. What deserves to be thrown out is the moral framing that shadows the whole subject: the assumption that owning is what grown-ups do and renting is what people do while they wait. Roughly half the households of Germany and Switzerland rent, prosperously, for life. The dinner-party certainty on both sides is a story about identity. The arithmetic is indifferent to identity. It only wants to know how long you are staying.
