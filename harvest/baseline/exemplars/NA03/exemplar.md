The exhibition was held in the atrium of the Marion Public Library, where the Friends of the Library sell used paperbacks off folding tables. Forty boards were arranged in a horseshoe. The visiting grandmaster, Anatoly Vitsin, had flown in from a tournament in Saint Louis, and the local club had covered his fee — $2,500, plus dinner — with a bake sale and a check from a dentist who plays the Najdorf badly but loyally.

Board 23 belonged to Ruth Castellano, 64, a retired hospital pharmacist rated 1712. She brought her own set, wooden, the knights' ears worn smooth, and a thermos of black tea. Her preparation consisted of deciding, weeks earlier, that she would not try to surprise anyone. She had played the Caro-Kann since 1987, and she intended to play it that night.

A simul is a strange contract. The grandmaster gives strength and takes time; the amateurs give time and take their one chance. Vitsin moved around the horseshoe at a walk, spending three or four seconds a board, and by move 12 he had won a pawn from Ruth with a small, familiar trick. The spectators drifted toward the boards where the games were already burning down. Hers looked finished the way a pot looks finished just before it boils over.

What Vitsin missed, on his eleventh circuit, was that Ruth had given the pawn back on purpose. Her rook stood behind a passed pawn that did not yet look passed. Pharmacists are careful with sequences; she had checked hers four times, waiting between his visits with her hands in her lap. On move 31 he took the offered pawn with his bishop, already turning toward Board 24, and she said, quietly, "Excuse me," and pushed the passer.

He stopped. He stood over the board for two full minutes, which in a simul is a public confession, and the spectators came back. There was no combination to find. The pawn cost the bishop, and the bishop was the game. Vitsin played six more moves at his walking pace, out of courtesy, then reached across the table and shook her hand. "The Caro-Kann," he said, as if that explained it, and in a way it did.

He finished the evening with 37 wins, two draws, one loss. Ruth stayed to help fold the tables. Someone urged her to frame the scoresheet, and she said she would think about it, but the scoresheet went into the bag with the wooden set, and there it stayed. She had the position by heart anyway. She still sets it up sometimes, plays the bishop capture for him, and pushes the pawn.
