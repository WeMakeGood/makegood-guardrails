Start with what sunlight actually is. The light leaving the sun looks white, but it is a mixture of every visible wavelength, from short blue waves of about 450 nanometers to red waves nearly twice as long. White is what that whole crowd looks like when it arrives together. Everything that follows comes from what happens when the crowd is pulled apart.

The thing that pulls it apart is air. The atmosphere is mostly nitrogen and oxygen, and each molecule is thousands of times smaller than a wavelength of visible light. When a light wave washes over a particle that small, the particle's electrons are jostled into oscillation, and an oscillating charge radiates — it re-emits the light in new directions. Physicists call this Rayleigh scattering, after Lord Rayleigh, who worked out its mathematics in 1871. A scattered wave has been knocked off the straight path from the sun and sent somewhere else in the sky.

Here is the step that decides everything: the scattering is not even-handed. The strength of the effect depends on the fourth power of frequency, which means short waves scatter far more than long ones. Blue light, with roughly twice the frequency of red, scatters about sixteen times as strongly. Red light mostly sails straight through the air; blue light gets batted around.

Now look up on a clear day, away from the sun. The light reaching your eye from that patch of empty sky did not come straight from the sun — nothing is there but air. It is sunlight that was traveling somewhere else entirely until a molecule redirected it toward you. And because blue scatters sixteen times more readily than red, the redirected light is overwhelmingly blue. The sky is blue because you are seeing the rejects, the short waves thrown out of the sun's beam by trillions of molecular collisions. (Violet scatters even more strongly, but the sun emits less of it and our eyes are poor at seeing it, so blue wins.)

A sunset is the same accounting read from the other side of the ledger. At noon, sunlight crosses one thickness of atmosphere to reach you. At sunset, coming in nearly horizontal, it may cross forty times as much air. Over that long path the scattering that merely tints the daytime sky becomes a systematic robbery: the blue is stripped out mile after mile and donated to other people's skies, the ones for whom it is still midday. What survives the trip is whatever the air could not easily deflect — orange and red, the long stubborn waves.

So the two colors are one phenomenon seen from two seats. The blue overhead is the scattered light. The red on the horizon is what remains after the scattering is done.
