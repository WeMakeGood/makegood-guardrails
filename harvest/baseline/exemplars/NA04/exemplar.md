Rain had been falling on Johnstown, Pennsylvania, for most of a day and a night when the morning of May 31, 1889, came up gray. Nobody in town found this alarming. Johnstown sat at the bottom of a steep Allegheny valley where two rivers met, and it flooded nearly every spring; merchants kept stock ready to move to the second floor, and families rolled up the parlor carpet and waited for the water to fall back. By mid-morning the rivers were over their banks, schools were letting out early, and boys were poling rafts of scrap lumber down the flooded streets. It was a nuisance. It was also an old one.

The trouble that morning was fourteen miles up the valley and 450 feet higher, where an earthen dam held back Lake Conemaugh, a two-mile sheet of water kept as a summer playground by the South Fork Fishing and Hunting Club. The club's members came out from Pittsburgh by rail — among them Andrew Carnegie, Henry Clay Frick, and Andrew Mellon, names that would later be carved over libraries and museum doors. The dam they lounged beside had been built decades earlier to serve a canal system that no longer existed. Its discharge pipes had been sold for scrap by a previous owner, so there was no way to lower the lake. Its crest had been cut down to let two carriages pass abreast on the road that ran across it, and its one spillway was screened with iron mesh to keep the club's stocked bass from escaping.

All that morning the lake climbed. The club's resident engineer, a 23-year-old named John Parke, watched laborers plow at the crest, trying to heighten it faster than the water rose, while another crew hacked at rock to open a second spillway. The fish screens choked with debris. Around noon, Parke rode to the village of South Fork so that a warning could be telegraphed down the valley: the dam might go. Warnings like it had come down in other wet springs and come to nothing, and in the towns below, this one was mostly shrugged off.

At ten minutes past three that afternoon, the dam did not burst so much as move aside. Twenty million tons of water left the lake and started down the Little Conemaugh toward Johnstown, a rolling wall of water and wreckage — trees, boxcars, whole houses — at times thirty-five feet high. The people bailing out their cellars below had less than an hour.
