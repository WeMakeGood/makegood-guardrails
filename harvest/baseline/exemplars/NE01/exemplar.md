The Cross-Valley Line resumed service Monday, ending a six-week shutdown that had closed the regional commuter railroad for bridge repairs.

The reopening returns trains to the line's riders six weeks after they lost them. It also closes the books on a project that finished $4 million over budget and drew criticism, before the trains came back, from the union that speaks for those riders.

The transit authority had budgeted $18 million for the repairs. The final cost was $22 million, an overrun of roughly a fifth. Lena Ortiz, a spokesperson for the authority, attributed the added cost to "unexpected corrosion" — deterioration beyond anything the authority had allowed for when it priced the job. By that account, the extra $4 million is what the corrosion cost: the difference between the job the authority planned and the job it got.

That explanation covers the money. It does not, on its face, speak to the calendar, and the calendar is where the project drew its other objection. The riders' union had criticized the length of the shutdown. The arithmetic behind the complaint is plain: 14,000 people ride the Cross-Valley Line each day, and for six weeks none of them could.

The $22 million is spent, the six weeks are over, and the trains are running again. The corrosion, at least, is no longer unexpected.
