Betamax was the better machine. Nearly every account of the format war begins there, and nearly every account draws the wrong lesson from it. The picture was marginally sharper — measurable in a lab, invisible on the 19-inch sets of 1976. On that sliver of superiority an entire mythology rests: that VHS won by cynicism, that the market punished excellence, that the pornography industry tipped the scales. The last claim is the most repeated and the least true; adult films were released on both formats.

What decided the war was an hour of tape. The cassette Sony shipped in 1975 recorded 60 minutes. The cassette JVC shipped a year later recorded two hours — enough for a movie, enough for a football game. The difference looks technical. It was a judgment about what the machine was for. Sony had built an instrument of fidelity and assumed buyers would prize what its engineers prized. JVC asked what a family would do with a recorder on a Sunday afternoon and sized the cassette to the answer. When RCA made four hours of recording — a full NFL broadcast — its condition for selling the machine in America, the VHS camp stretched the format again and took the deal. Sony treated running time as a constraint to be apologized for. Its rivals treated it as the product.

Everything else in the standard account is downstream of that judgment. JVC licensed the format widely — Matsushita, Hitachi, Sharp, RCA — so VHS machines were cheaper and on more shelves. Rental stores, when they arrived, stocked the format more households owned, and the loop closed. These network effects were real, and by the mid-1980s they were doing most of the work. But feedback loops amplify an existing lead; they do not create one. The lead was built in the first two years, when a customer standing in a showroom had to choose between a machine that could record the Sunday game and one that could record half of it. Sony extended Betamax to two hours by 1977, and further after that. It did not matter. The tilt had begun, and every later improvement arrived on both formats roughly at par.

The lesson usually extracted — that the better product lost — gets the verdict backwards. Betamax was the better machine only by the measure Sony chose. Measured by the job customers were actually hiring a recorder to do, VHS was better from the day it shipped. The market did not fail to recognize quality. It recognized it precisely, and it was not where Sony was looking.
