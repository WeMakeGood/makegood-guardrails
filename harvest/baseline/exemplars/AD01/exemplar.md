Everyone knows it, and it isn't true. The Great Wall is long, but from orbit length is the wrong measurement. Visibility depends on width and contrast, and the wall offers little of either: about 30 feet across at its grandest, built of stone and rammed earth the same dun color as the hills it follows. Astronauts have looked. Yang Liwei, China's first man in space, admitted in 2003 that he could not find it, an answer so deflating that Beijing moved to correct its own textbooks. What everyone knows, then, is a claim that outlived every chance of checking it. An English antiquarian ventured it in 1754, Ripley's Believe It or Not made it famous in 1932, and for the next three decades nobody could go and see. By the time someone could, the fact had been printed too often to die. It also flatters a natural intuition: the biggest thing people ever built ought to be visible from the biggest vantage there is. But highways are wider than the wall, and nobody claims to see those.
