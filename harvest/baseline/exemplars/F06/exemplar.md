Both approaches begin from the same promise: the home that was affordable for this buyer should be affordable for the next one. The difference is where the promise lives.

A deed restriction places the promise in a document. A covenant recorded against the title caps the resale price or restricts the buyer pool, and the restriction rides along with the property. It is inexpensive to create, requires no ongoing institution, and for a funder who wants affordability terms attached to a subsidy, it is the simplest instrument available. Those are real advantages, and the board should weigh them as such.

But a document only binds; it does not attend. Between sales, a deed restriction is silent. Enforcement happens — if it happens — at the moment of resale, and depends on someone noticing the covenant, reading it correctly, and holding the line against a seller with every incentive to test it. Restrictions are also commonly written with expiration dates, so the affordability they secure is a term, not a condition. When the term runs out, the home returns to the market and the public investment in it converts to private gain.

Harbor Bend's model places the promise in a structure instead. The trust owns the land permanently; the homeowner owns the home on a 99-year renewable ground lease; and the resale formula — the seller receives their original purchase price plus 25% of the appraised appreciation — is administered by a steward who is party to every transaction, holding an option to purchase or to approve the next income-qualified buyer. Nothing expires, and no one has to notice anything: the trust is already in the room.

The record shows what that presence produces. Nineteen resales since 2014, and every resold home remained affordable under the formula. Sellers left with an average equity gain of $28,400 — the formula asks them to share appreciation, not to forfeit it. And the outcomes a covenant cannot touch at all: zero foreclosures in the trust's history, and three hardship interventions resolved with the household still housed. A recorded restriction has no mechanism for keeping a struggling family in their home. A steward does.

The honest cost of our model is the steward itself — staff, budget, an institution that must be sustained for the promise to hold. A deed restriction is cheaper because it does less. The question for the board is never which instrument is more efficient on paper. It is which one is still working thirty years from now.
