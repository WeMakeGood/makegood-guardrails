NIAGARA FALLS, Oct. 25 — Annie Edson Taylor, a schoolteacher sixty-three years of age, went over Niagara Falls in a barrel yesterday and lived. No one before her had ever gone over the falls in a barrel and come out alive. She is the first, and as of this writing she is the only one.

She was bruised when she came out of the barrel. She was also alive, and the bruises are the worst that has been reported of her condition. For a woman who had just been carried over Niagara Falls, that is a short accounting of injuries.

What sent a schoolteacher of sixty-three over the falls was not misadventure. She went deliberately, and she went in the hope of fame and of money. Whether the plunge will bring her either is a question that cannot be answered today. It has already brought her the one distinction no one else holds, which is to have survived it.

Having made the trip once, Taylor does not propose to make it a second time. Since her plunge she has said plainly that she would not do it again. She has gone further than that: she has warned others against attempting it at all. On this point her advice admits of no exceptions. The first person to go over Niagara Falls in a barrel and live asks not to be followed.

The record, then, stands as follows. On October 24, 1901, a sixty-three-year-old schoolteacher entered a barrel, went over Niagara Falls in it, and emerged bruised but alive — the first person to survive the feat. A day later she is recovering from her bruises, waiting on the fame and the money she went over for, and counseling everyone else to stay out of the barrel.
