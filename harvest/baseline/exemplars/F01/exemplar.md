# The land stays. The homes stay affordable.

Harbor Bend Community Land Trust owns land — permanently. Residents own the homes on it, hold their own mortgages, and sign a 99-year renewable ground lease. When a homeowner sells, they receive their original purchase price plus 25% of the home's appreciation, and the home passes to the next income-qualified buyer at a price that buyer can afford. That is the whole model. Since 2011, it has put 62 homes in Harbor Bend permanently out of the speculative market.

## How it's going

Since 2014, 19 of our homes have resold. Every one stayed affordable. Sellers left with an average of $28,400 in equity — real wealth, built in a home that remains within reach of the next family. Our residents stay a median of 7.4 years, in a county where the median rental tenure is 2.9. We have never had a foreclosure. Three households have hit hardship serious enough to need a lease-based intervention; all three are still housed.

## What we've built

Of the 62 homes, we acquired and rehabilitated 41 and built 21 new — the newest finished in spring 2026. In 2023 we bought an eight-unit apartment building on Alder Street. All eight resident households stayed in place through the purchase and rehab, rents are held at their prior levels by covenant for three years, and the building converts to resident ownership by 2028.

Not everything has worked. Our down-payment matching pilot (2019–2021) drew six applications for every one we could fund, and administering the match consumed 18% of the fund itself. We paused it and put the remaining balance toward Alder Street.

## Who we are

Three full-time staff, about forty volunteers, and a nine-member board — five seats held by residents and community members, plus two non-voting youth observer seats our first Youth Stewardship cohort proposed themselves. Dana Whitfield (they/them) has led the trust since its founding. More than half of our roughly $1.1M budget is earned revenue, not fundraising.
