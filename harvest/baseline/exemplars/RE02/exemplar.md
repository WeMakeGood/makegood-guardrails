On paper, the choice looks like arithmetic. Paying down a 6 percent loan is a guaranteed 6 percent return; the stock market has averaged more than that over long stretches, so the spreadsheet says invest. The spreadsheet is not wrong, exactly. It just leaves out most of what decides the question.

Start with the word the spreadsheet skips: guaranteed. The 6 percent from paying down the loan arrives with certainty, in every market, in every year. The market's higher average is payment for accepting the possibility of much worse — there have been ten-year stretches when a diversified portfolio returned less than nothing. If this household will need the money within five or seven years, the guarantee should dominate. If the horizon is twenty-five years, the odds shift heavily toward investing, though odds are all they are.

Taxes complicate both sides. If the loan is a mortgage and the household itemizes, the interest may be deductible, and the true cost of the debt is something less than 6 percent. Investment returns, meanwhile, get taxed on the way out — unless they don't. An employer 401(k) match is the trump card here: a dollar matched at fifty cents earns 50 percent before the market does anything at all. No debt payoff beats that. Whatever else the household decides, the first dollars should capture the match.

Then there is liquidity, the factor people discover too late. Money sent to the lender is gone. Home equity is famously hard to get back out, and the bank still expects next month's payment regardless of how much principal has been retired. A household that puts the whole $50,000 against the loan and then loses a paycheck has bought neither safety nor flexibility — it has a smaller balance and an empty account. Investments, whatever their risks, can be sold on a Tuesday. If there is no emergency fund, some of this money is not really available for either option.

Two quieter factors deserve a seat. One is inflation: a fixed-rate loan gets cheaper in real terms every year prices rise, which argues, mildly, for keeping it. The other is temperament, which is not a soft consideration but a predictive one. A household that will panic-sell in the next downturn will not earn the market's average; it will earn the average minus its worst instincts. For that household, the boring 6 percent is the better investment. And some people simply sleep better without debt. Sleep has a price, and it is allowed to be worth some basis points.

So the honest answer is a sequence, not a verdict. Capture any employer match. Hold back a real emergency reserve. Then compare the loan's after-tax rate to what the household can plausibly expect — and plausibly tolerate — from markets. At 6 percent, the guaranteed return and the expected one sit close enough that the deciding factors are the ones the spreadsheet cannot hold: the horizon, the job security, the stomach. A 2 percent loan makes the choice easy. A 6 percent loan makes it a mirror.
