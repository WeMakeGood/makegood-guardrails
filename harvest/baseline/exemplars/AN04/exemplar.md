The case for the transistor is easy to make. More of them have been manufactured than any other object in human history, and billions ride in any pocket. They underwrite nearly everything the present runs on: computation, communication, navigation, the pacemaker, the power grid. Switch the transistors off and the modern world stops.

But "stops" is the wrong test. A heart stops without blood; that does not make blood the reason you exist. The question is not what the modern world runs on. It is what made the world modern in the first place, and the answer is the printing press.

Consider Europe around 1450. Knowledge was scarce and unstable: a book was a year of a copyist's labor, and every copy drifted from the last, errors compounding like rumor. Fifty years after Gutenberg, presses were operating in more than 250 towns and had put an estimated 20 million volumes into circulation. Scarcity was over. What replaced it was something stranger and more consequential: the fixed text. For the first time, a thousand readers in a thousand places could examine the same words, the same tables, the same diagrams — and write back.

Everything we call modern grows from that. Cumulative science depends on it, because a result that cannot be reproduced exactly cannot be checked; it is no accident that Copernicus and Vesalius both published in 1543, within a century of movable type. The Reformation was a press phenomenon — Luther's theses crossed Germany in weeks, not decades, and no bishop could recall them. Printed vernaculars hardened into national languages, and national languages into nations. The newspaper assembled strangers into that novel thing, a public. Even mass literacy, which we imagine as print's precondition, was its product: people learned to read because there was suddenly something to read.

The transistor is a magnificent heir to all this. It made print's products instant, searchable, and nearly free to move, and it extended them to domains no press could reach. But it inherited its purposes rather than inventing them. It moves information whose authority print established, to publics print assembled, in languages print standardized. Amplification, even at miraculous degree, is not origination. The press did not speed up the medieval world; it replaced that world's whole way of knowing. The transistor sped up the one the press had built.

The transistor's own biography concedes the point. It was invented in 1947 at Bell Labs, an industrial laboratory of the kind that print-made science made possible, then announced in a physics journal, secured by a printed patent, and propagated through textbooks. Every transistor entered the world the way modern things do: published first.
