# The Alder Street Acquisition

**Harbor Bend Community Land Trust · Kettle County · 2023**

In 2023, Harbor Bend Community Land Trust paid $1.9 million for an eight-unit apartment building on Alder Street. The trust's entire annual budget is roughly $1.1 million.

That gap is the first question peer organizations ask, and it is the right one. Harbor Bend is a small operation: three full-time staff, about forty active volunteers, sixty-two permanently affordable homes stewarded since its founding in 2011. Its model is individual homeownership on trust-owned land — a 99-year renewable ground lease, a resale formula that returns each seller's purchase price plus 25 percent of appraised appreciation. An occupied rental building sits differently in that model. Its eight households were tenants, not buyers, and no ground lease was waiting for them at closing.

The purchase was assembled from three sources: a $600,000 grant from a public housing trust fund, $1.05 million in CDFI financing, and $250,000 from the trust's reserves. Rehabilitation after closing added $340,000, bringing the full commitment to $2.24 million — roughly twice the organization's annual operating budget.

Part of that money runs through a failure the trust does not dress up. Its down-payment matching pilot, launched in 2019, was paused in 2021 after applications outran the fund six to one and administering the match consumed 18 percent of the fund itself. Rather than relaunch a program that couldn't keep pace with its own demand, the board redirected the remaining balance toward Alder Street. A fund that could help households one match at a time went instead toward a building that holds eight households at once. The trust describes the pause plainly, and funders evaluating similar organizations should treat that plainness as a signal: an organization that will name what didn't work is easier to underwrite than one that won't.

What the acquisition bought, above all, was continuity. All eight resident households stayed in place through both the purchase and the rehabilitation. Rents are held at their prior levels by covenant for three years. The building converts to resident ownership by 2028.

For an organization this size to carry a deal this size, the credible answer is its record, and Harbor Bend's is specific: nineteen resales since 2014, every one of them still affordable to the next buyer under the formula; an average seller equity gain of $28,400; zero foreclosures in the trust's history, with three lease-based hardship interventions all resolved with the household still housed; a median resident tenure of 7.4 years against a county median rental tenure of 2.9. None of those numbers is about Alder Street. All of them are about whether the steward of Alder Street keeps its commitments.

Peers considering a similar acquisition should also see clearly what is not yet proven. The verifiable outcomes to date are early ones: no household displaced, rents held, rehabilitation complete. The outcome the building was purchased for — eight tenant households becoming owners — is scheduled, not achieved. The rent covenant runs three years; the conversion deadline is 2028. The honest way to read this case in 2026 is as a successful acquisition and a conversion still in progress, and organizations telling their own version of this story should resist collapsing the two.

The structural lesson is transferable. A small trust did not get big to do this deal. It layered public money that expected affordability, mission-aligned debt that expected repayment, and its own reserves — including the remains of a program it had the discipline to stop — and it put its record, rather than its size, in front of the lender. What Alder Street tests next is whether the same steadiness carries eight renters across the line into ownership.
