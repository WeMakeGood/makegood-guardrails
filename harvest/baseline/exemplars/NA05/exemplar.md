The ground came up gray, but gray was an incomplete word for it. Directly below the window it was ash. Out toward the horizon, where the sun sat low, it ran to tan and cocoa, and down-sun, behind the spacecraft, it went nearly white. The color moved when the eye moved. Nothing else did.

Every crater held its shadow the way a cup holds coffee, black and full to the rim. There was no gradation at the edge, no band where dark shaded into light; the boundary was a drawn line. Rocks the size of medicine balls threw shadows sharper than the rocks themselves, and the smallest craters, no wider than a hand, were stamped into the surface as crisply as the big ones, the same shape at every scale, so the eye had nothing to measure with. A boulder on a rise might be ten feet off or a thousand. The horizon looked close enough to walk to before lunch, and it curved, visibly, like the deck of a ship.

The soil was the strangest part. It was not sand. It lay smooth as poured plaster wherever nothing had disturbed it, and where the descent engine had scoured it, lighter streaks fanned outward in rays. A boot came down and the print took every detail of the tread, clean at the edges, half an inch deep, and stopped there. The dust did not billow. Kicked, it flew out flat and fast in a thin sheet and dropped all at once, each grain landing where its arc ended, no cloud, no drift, no haze settling afterward. It had been lying there, smooth, holding the shape of the last thing that touched it, since long before there was anyone anywhere to come and look.
