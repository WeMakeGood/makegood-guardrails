# Executive Summary

This spring, Harbor Bend Community Land Trust completed its 62nd permanently affordable home. The number matters less than what stands behind it: fifteen years of a model that works the same way every time. The trust owns the land; residents own the homes on it, under 99-year renewable ground leases. When a homeowner sells, they receive their original purchase price plus 25% of the appraised appreciation — and the home stays affordable for the next buyer. Nineteen homes have resold since 2014. All nineteen remained affordable. The average seller left with $28,400 in equity.

Stability is the outcome we watch most closely. Median tenure among our residents is 7.4 years, against a county median rental tenure of 2.9. In the trust's history there have been zero foreclosures — and three hardship interventions under the lease, each resolved with the household still housed.

The Alder Street building, an 8-unit acquisition completed in 2023, remains on schedule. All eight resident households stayed in place through purchase and rehabilitation, rents are held at prior levels by covenant, and the building converts to resident ownership by 2028. Our Youth Stewardship Program graduated its second cohort of 14 in 2025; the two youth observer seats on our board came from the first cohort's own proposal, and those seats continue to sharpen our governance.

One candid note: the down-payment matching pilot remains paused. Demand outran the fund six to one, and administration consumed 18% of the fund itself. We redirected the balance toward Alder Street, and we will not relaunch the pilot until we can run it well.

The trust operates on roughly $1.1 million annually, 55% of it earned revenue — a foundation that lets three staff, about forty volunteers, and a board where residents and community members hold five of nine seats keep doing patient work at a durable pace.
