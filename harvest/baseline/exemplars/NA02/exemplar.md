The vendors get the best hour to themselves. By seven the trucks are backed against the curb, tailgates down, and the stalls go up in a clatter of pipe and canvas that carries across the empty lot. A woman in a fleece vest stacks apples in a spiral, finds a bruised one, sets it aside without looking at it. The bread man arranges nothing. His loaves come out of the van in the wooden trays they cooled in, and by ten the trays will be empty.

The cold holds on along the shaded side of the lot and gives up everywhere else. Low sun comes in over the fence and catches the last of the tomatoes, fewer this week and cheaper, crowded to one end of a table that belongs mostly to squash now: butternut, delicata, acorn, a pyramid of pie pumpkins with a hand-lettered sign. The dahlias are the size of dinner plates. They stand all morning in white buckets, waiting to be chosen.

Customers come in two waves. First the serious ones, alone, with canvas bags and exact plans, who lift things and smell them and do not chat. Then, toward nine, everyone else — strollers, dogs on long leads, a line at the coffee cart that bends around the honey stand. A farmer weighs peppers, thumbs the tare button, rounds down. A toddler is handed a ground cherry and looks betrayed, then amazed.

By late morning the sun has finished its work and the jackets come off, tied around waists. The apple woman's spiral has slumped into a heap, and she has stopped rebuilding it. What remains on the tables looks picked-over and is; what's gone rides home in bags, on handlebars, under stroller seats. The lot will be a parking lot again by one.
