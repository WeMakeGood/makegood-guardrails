Rental subsidy is a treadmill. The public money arrives, the rent comes down, the family stays — and the whole arrangement holds only as long as the money keeps arriving. When the contract expires or the funding cycle turns, the unit snaps back to market and the affordability vanishes as if it had never been there. Nothing was built. The dollars bought time, not housing.

There is another way to spend the same dollar, and a small land trust in the coastal town of Harbor Bend has spent fifteen years demonstrating it.

Harbor Bend Community Land Trust doesn't subsidize anyone's rent. It owns land, permanently, and the people who live on that land own the homes standing on it, under a 99-year renewable ground lease. When an owner sells, a formula in the lease does the work a subsidy would otherwise have to do forever: the seller receives their original purchase price plus a quarter of the home's appreciation, and the home passes to the next income-qualified buyer at a price that stays within reach. The affordability isn't purchased again. It's already in the deed.

The trust has stewarded 62 homes this way since 2011. Nineteen have resold since 2014, and every one remained affordable at resale — nineteen affordable homes that required no second round of funding, no renewed contract, no line item in anyone's budget. The sellers didn't leave empty-handed, either. On average, they walked away with $28,400 in equity, which is $28,400 more than any rent subsidy has ever left behind.

And people stay. Median tenure in a Harbor Bend home is 7.4 years; the median renter in surrounding Kettle County moves after 2.9. In the trust's history, not one home has been lost to foreclosure.

A subsidy lowers the rent this month. Permanence changes what the home is — for every household that will ever live in it.
