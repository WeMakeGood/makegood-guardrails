Consider what a first job used to consist of. Not the tasks, which were mostly menial, but the surround: watching a senior colleague soften a hostile email before sending it, overhearing how a partner priced a project, getting pulled into a meeting for no better reason than sitting at the nearest desk. None of that appeared in a job description. Nearly all of it was the job.

The claim that remote work has been better for junior employees gets the ledger backwards. Remote work has transferred advantage upward, from the people who most needed the office to the people who had already taken what they needed from it.

Seniority is stored capital. A twenty-year veteran brings to her kitchen table a network that returns her calls, a reputation that enters the meeting before she does, and a settled judgment about what matters and what can slide. Remote work lets her spend that capital in comfort. The commute is gone; the standing remains. Her stock was accumulated years ago, in rooms.

A junior employee has no stock. What a junior needs is apprenticeship, and apprenticeship is precisely what the video call fails to transmit. A scheduled call carries the agenda and nothing else. The correction offered in passing, the draft glimpsed over a shoulder, the offhand "you should talk to Marcus about this" — these travel through proximity or they do not travel at all. Managers once had a name for the office version, management by walking around, and its logic cut both ways: while the manager was watching, the junior was watching back, learning what competence looked like at close range. There is no walking around a Slack workspace.

The case for the claim deserves a fair hearing. Remote work did spare juniors some of the bottom rung's indignities: the open-plan surveillance, the rent within commuting distance of a headquarters city, the nightly performance of staying late. For a young worker caring for a parent, or living far from the expensive hubs where careers begin, these gains are real. They are also the wrong currency. Flexibility is worth most to people whose careers can already absorb it. What a senior employee experiences as autonomy, a junior experiences as silence: no signal that the work is right, no sponsor registering the effort, no view of how the good ones do it.

And the bill arrives late, which is what makes it easy to deny. A junior who spends three remote years producing adequate work on schedule will look, on every dashboard, like a success. Dashboards measure output. They do not measure the judgment that was never formed, the ally never acquired, the promotion that goes instead to someone who happened to share a hallway with the decision-maker. By the time the difference shows, it reads as a difference in talent.

The office was never kind to junior people. It was merely indispensable to them, and its indispensability stayed invisible until it lapsed. Senior employees got their time back, and it cost them almost nothing. Junior employees got a laptop, a login, and a closed door.
