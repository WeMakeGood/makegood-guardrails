That was easy to give, and you should distrust it at the price you paid. I don't know what you do, what it pays, who counts on the paycheck, or what you'd be walking toward once you finished walking out. An answer that needs none of that isn't advice; it's a coin flip with better manners.

But the question tells me something the answer can't. People who are basically fine at work don't ask strangers for permission to leave it. They ask about raises, about transfers, about a manager they can't read. You skipped all of that and went straight to the exit, which suggests the deciding is mostly done and what you want now is a second signature.

So here is a smaller test than quitting. Tonight, write the resignation letter. Date it, address it, say what you would actually say. Then put it in a drawer and leave it alone for two weeks. If the letter reads like relief every time you look at it, you have your yes, and it will be yours rather than mine. If instead you catch yourself revising it, softening a line, adding a condition, wondering whether Thursday's meeting changes anything, then you have learned the truer thing: you don't want to quit your job. You want your job to be different. That is a harder question, and a better one, and nobody can answer it in one word.
