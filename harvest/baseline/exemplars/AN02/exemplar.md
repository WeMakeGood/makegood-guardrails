Cities rarely get a second chance at land. A downtown parking lot is one — a flat, paid-off mistake at the center of everything, holding a hundred cars a day while the neighborhoods around it hold waiting lists. The city should build housing on it, mixed-income and at real density, and it should be honest about what that decision buries.

The case begins with who is missing. The teacher who commutes forty minutes from the only suburb she can afford. The line cook who leaves at eleven and pays for the ride because the last bus left at ten. The retired couple who sold the house and discovered that downtown, the one place they could walk to everything, had nothing for them under $2,800 a month. A downtown is not short of admirers; it is short of residents. Three hundred homes on three acres — a third of them below market rate, the rest helping to carry that third — put people where the jobs, the buses, and the pharmacies already are. Every other answer to that problem starts with land the city does not own.

The park deserves better than to be brushed aside. It is the more lovable proposal, and the loss it names is real. Housing forecloses. The day the foundation is poured, that ground is spoken for — not for a budget cycle but for a century. The children who grow up in those apartments will want a green place within a block, and it will not be there. A city can always build housing somewhere; it can almost never carve open space out of a finished downtown. Whoever says otherwise has not tried to buy back a city block. That is the true cost, and it does not amortize.

But consider who a downtown park serves when no one lives beside it. It fills at lunch, empties at six, and spends its nights as scenery people drive past on their way home — home being, increasingly, somewhere else. A park is a common good only where there is a commons: people close enough to use it on an ordinary Tuesday. Build the housing and the constituency for green space arrives with it — three hundred households who will pack the public meetings until the city narrows a street, plants a pocket park, claims the next vacant corner. Build the park first and the constituency stays forty minutes away.

The lot offers one chance to decide what the center of the city is for. Grass would make it prettier. Homes would make it a place — lights on at ten, strollers at eight, someone there to notice whether the trees get planted at all. Choose the people. Then owe them the park, and pay the debt.
