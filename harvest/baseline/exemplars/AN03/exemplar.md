Order the soup. Whatever else happens at The Copper Kettle, and plenty else will, the soup justifies the reservation. The kitchen makes three each day and posts them on a card by the door like a parish notice. Over two visits I tried five: a smoked-onion broth deepened with rye crumbs, a white bean soup finished with fennel oil and enough black pepper to argue with, and a chilled sorrel soup, cold and sour and improbably green, that I have thought about since. These are built the slow way, on stocks that taste of hours, and they arrive at a temperature someone has actually checked. A restaurant that can do this can, in principle, do anything.

The Copper Kettle does not do anything else. The mains read as if written by the soup cook's ambitious cousin and prepared by nobody in particular. A half chicken came dry at the breast and slack at the skin, over a mustard cream that had split into weeping curds. A hanger steak ordered medium rare arrived gray through, and its replacement, twenty minutes later, arrived gray through. The fries were cold on both visits, which begins to look like policy.

Service compounds the trouble. Our waiter was friendly and largely theoretical, surfacing at long intervals to apologize for the intervals. Water went unpoured, a wine order evaporated between the table and the bar, and the check required two requests and a wave. The room works against everyone in it: low ceiling, bare brick, and a playlist loud enough to reduce conversation to semaphore. Dessert is three items, all involving caramel, a list that suggests exhaustion rather than restraint. The pot de crème tasted mostly of the refrigerator it came from.

The prices assume none of this is happening. At $34, that chicken is billed as the work of a kitchen firing on every burner; the soups, at $12, are the only honest numbers on the menu.

So the verdict splits. As a restaurant, The Copper Kettle is a muddle, careless in the kitchen and adrift on the floor, and at these prices the carelessness is hard to forgive. As a soup counter with delusions of grandeur, it is nearly great. Sit at the bar, order two soups and the good sourdough, and leave before the entrées can find you. Whoever is tending those kettles deserves a better restaurant, and on the evidence of the bowls alone, could build one.
