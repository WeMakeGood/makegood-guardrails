The last movie ever rented from Video Haven is Out of the Past, the 1947 one, Robert Mitchum and Jane Greer, and Dale Kowalczyk waives the fee. "Bring it back whenever," he tells Marcy Duttweiler, who has held membership card number 00114 since the first Bush administration. "Or don't." It is a little after nine on Saturday morning, twelve hours before he locks the door for good, and the store is already fuller than it has been in years.

Everything is for sale. Tapes are five for a dollar, DVDs a quarter each, and the shelving itself is spoken for — a man from a salvage yard in Terre Haute is coming Monday with a flatbed. The neon sign in the front window, a reel of film unspooling into the word HAVEN, went last week to a bar in Columbus for eight hundred dollars. What people are buying today, Kowalczyk suspects, is proof: that the store was real, and that there was a time in Ashford when Friday night began in these four aisles.

He opened the place in 1987, in a storefront on Ninth Street that had been a shoe-repair shop, with 600 tapes and a loan co-signed by his father-in-law. At its peak Video Haven stocked 14,000 titles and employed eleven people, most of them teenagers who went on to become teachers and nurses and, in one case, a location scout in Atlanta who still sends a Christmas card. Blockbuster arrived out by the highway in 1994 and left in 2011; Kowalczyk outlasted it by carrying what the chain wouldn't — foreign films, horror deep cuts, a documentary section he curated himself. Streaming was a different kind of opponent. It didn't compete for his customers so much as dissolve the habit that produced them. The store's revenue chart for the past decade looks less like a collapse than a long exhalation.

He kept it open four years past the point a sensible man would have quit, because of maybe two hundred regulars: the retirees, the film students from the community college, the parents who wanted their kids to pick a movie by holding it. Near the register this morning, a boy of about eight turns a copy of Jaws over in his hands and asks his mother, seriously, how the movie gets out of the box. Kowalczyk hears this and does not smile. He reaches under the counter for the tape rewinder, one of the last working ones in town, and says, "Come here. I'll show you."
